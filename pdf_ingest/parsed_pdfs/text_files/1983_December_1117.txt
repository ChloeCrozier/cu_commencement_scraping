HONORARY DEGREE 
DOCTOR OF HUMANITIES 
MAUDE E. CALLEN 
Maude Callen has devoted her entire professional life as a nurse midwife to 
caring for the sick and needy in the backwoods of rural Berkeley County, South 
Carolina. For sixty years she has tried to 'be all things to her people: nurse, 
teacher, psychologist, dietician, friend and comforter. 
Born eighty-five years ago in Quincy, Florida, Mrs. Callen was orphaned at 
age four and was raised by an aunt and uncle. In 1922 she was graduated from 
The Florida A&M College and received her nurse's training at the Georgia Infirmary at Savannah. 
Mrs. Callen came to Berkeley County in 1923 as a United Thank Offering 
worker and missionary nurse for the Protestant Episcopal Church. She found 
there a tremendous need for health care among the people in the remote areas 
of the county, and it made a great impression on this young nurse just beginning 
a life of service. 
Settling in the Pineville community, she traveled by wagon and cart over 
nearly impassable dirt roads to treat the sick and needy. Two rooms in her small 
home served as a clinic. She was 22 miles from the county hospital and 10 miles 
from the nearest doctor. 
When people became ill, they usually went for "Miss Maude," who always was 
available day and night, seven days a week. Her ,workday often was as long as 
16 hours. Making a house call on a cold winter's night to deliver a baby, Mrs. 
Callen would build a fire in a pot in the wagon for ,warmth. She has been to 
homes which were impossible to reach even by wagon and had to cross ditches 
and creeks by foot, by walking on lo_gs. 
In 1936 she was employed by the Berkeley County Health Department as a 
public health nurse. She ran the first prenatal clinic and veneral disease clinic in 
the county with the assistance of a private physician. 
National attention was brought to her good works in 1951 when Life Magazine 
featured this remarkable individual in an article entitled "Nurse Midwife; Maude 
Callen Eases Pain of Birth, Life and Death." In photograph after photograph, Mrs. 
Callen was shown on her daily rounds going into homes, delivering babies, and 
caring for the aged, the sick, and the blind. 
In 1969 in a book of his "Photographs and Notes," the author of the article, 
Eugene Smith, had been so moved by what he had seen that he wrote the following comments about what it had meant to him personally to have met "Miss 
Maude." 
"This essay on the nurse midwife, Maude Callen, is in many ways, the most 
rewarding experience photography has allowed me. In the most special way, she 
is probably the greatest person I have ever been privileged to know; combining 
marvelous wisdom and compassion, a strength of true humility and true pride, 
all given direction through knowledge and purpose in a sheerly beautiful balance." 
When the nation read about Mrs. Callen, donations poured in from many 
people who wanted to stand up and cheer for her and to express their support 
for her mission. She used the $27,000 in gifts to fulfill a longtime dream. She 
built a modern clinic in her home area of the Pineville community. 
If ever someone has lived a life which exemplifies the true meaning of the 
word "humanitarian," it is "Miss Maude." Her remarkable life of service to 
others is an outstanding example for all of us to reflect on in this modern world 
where purpose and meaning are sometimes difficult to define in the rush of 
daily living. 
Clemson University holds her and her accomplishments in the highest regard, 
and it is a special privilege for all of us at Clemson to honor her in this way.

CLEMSON ALMA MATER 
A. C. Corcoran, '19 
Where the Blue Ridge yawns its greatness, 
Where the Tigers play; 
Here the sons of dear old Clemson 
Reign supreme alway. 
Chorus 
Dear old Clemson, we will triumph 
And with all our might, 
That the Tiger's roar may echo 
O'er the mountain height. 
We will dream of great conquests, 
For our past is grand, 
And her sons have fought and conquered 
Every foreign land. 
Where the mountains smile in grandeur 
O'er the hill and dale, 
Here the Tigers lair is nestling 
Swept by storm and gale. 
We are brothers strong in manhood, 
For we work and strive; 
And our Alma Mater reigneth 
Ever in our lives.
